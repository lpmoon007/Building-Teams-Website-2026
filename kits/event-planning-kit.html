Tool 1Get the yes

The one-page approval pitch

Most good events die in the approval step, not the planning. Fill this in and send it to whoever controls the budget. It answers their three silent questions: why now, what changes, and what it costs.

1. The goal — what should be measurably different after this event?Pick one. "Have fun" isn't a goal a budget-holder can say yes to; "help a newly merged team trust each other" is.
2. Headcount
3. Target date / window
4. Location or venue
5. Estimated investmentper person × headcount
6. The business outcome it supportsTick what this event is really for — this is the language your sponsor cares about.
Retention / engagement
Onboarding a new or merged team
Cross-team collaboration
Leadership alignment
Culture / morale reset
CSR / community impact
7. The one sentence for your sponsorCopy this, fill the blanks, paste it into the email.
"We're investing about $______ to ______________________ so that our team ______________________ — and every dollar also builds something we donate to kids in the community."
8. The give-back angleThe detail that makes a "yes" easy: what your team builds and who receives it.
Tool 2Don't drop a detail

The 8-week countdown checklist

Work backward from the date. Tick as you go — this is the list that keeps a great idea from unraveling in the last week.

8 weeks out

Goal + headcount + budget approved (Tool 1)
Date locked with key leaders' calendars
Format chosen and provider briefed
Venue / room held

4 weeks out

Invite / save-the-date sent
A/V, power and layout confirmed with venue
Catering / breaks ordered
Accessibility + dietary needs collected

2 weeks out

Final headcount to provider & caterer
Run-of-show shared with leaders (Tool 3)
A leader briefed to open the event
Photographer / phone photos assigned

Week of & day-of

Reminder + logistics email to attendees
Room set 60 min early; A/V tested
Someone owns the welcome + the close
Follow-up note + photos planned for after
The detail people forget: plan the close, not just the open. The last five minutes — a leader naming what happened and why it mattered — is what people carry back to their desks.
Tool 3Run the day

The run-of-show template

Every good event has a minute-by-minute spine. Fill this in, share it with your venue and your provider, and nothing is left to chance on the day.
