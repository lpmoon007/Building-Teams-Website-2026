Learning Maze

By James Carter · Updated July 2026

The only way to find the path is to step wrong first — a quiet exercise in how your team treats mistakes and the people who find them.

Group size8–36 people
EnergyLow
Time45–60 min (incl. debrief)
SettingIndoors or outdoors
Best for
CoachingCommunication

By James Carter, founder of Building Teams — 25+ years designing team-building experiences for hundreds of leadership teams. Updated July 2026.

See it in action

What it is

Learning Maze is a deceptively simple grid of stones laid out on the floor, with a single hidden path running across it. The team’s job is to get everyone to the other side — but the only way to discover the path is to step on the wrong stones first. A wrong step “beeps.” The catch that makes this activity so revealing is the paradox at its centre: the beeps are mistakes, and yet the beeps are the only information the team ever gets. There is no way to learn the route except by getting it wrong, out loud, in front of everyone.

It is a low-energy, low-stakes exercise on the surface — nobody is lifting or racing anything — and teams usually have a very good time balancing their way through. Underneath, it is one of the sharpest mirrors we have for how a group treats its own mistakes and the people who happen to make them.

How it works

Stones are laid out in a grid, and the team lines up at one edge. One person at a time steps onto a stone; if it beeps, they have found a mistake, and they retrace their steps back off the grid so the next person can try. The path is only ever revealed one wrong — or right — step at a time, and nobody may mark the stones or use props to remember it, so the group has to hold the emerging map together, out loud. A storyline (a temple maze on the way to hidden treasure) and small penalties for stepping wrong on the way back keep the energy up. (Full step-by-step setup, the answer grids and the complete debrief questions are in the downloadable guide below.)

What it reveals: how a team treats its mistakes

This is the heart of Learning Maze. The whole task depends on people making mistakes and reporting them — and yet almost every group instinctively tries to avoid the beeps, even though the beeps are the only thing that moves them forward. Watch what happens to the person who steps wrong. Do teammates groan, or do they lean in because that person just handed the group a free piece of the map? Does the team quietly treat its boldest explorers — the ones willing to step on an unknown stone — as liabilities, or as its most valuable members? The parallel to work writes itself: organizations say they want people to take risks and surface problems early, then react to every stumble as a failure. Learning Maze makes that contradiction impossible to hide.

What it reveals: coaching and communication

Only one person can be on the grid at a time, which means everyone else is coaching from the edge. The group has to agree on which stone to try next, get that decision to the person standing on the maze, and keep a shared memory of the path alive without writing anything down. How does the team gather input — does it vote, defer to one voice, or dissolve into everyone calling out a different direction? Does the person on the stones actually get clear, usable guidance, or contradictory noise? And how does the group support the individual out there taking the risk on its behalf? These are the same coaching muscles a manager uses every day: giving someone the information they need to succeed, in a form they can act on, and backing them while they do it.

Interesting things to watch for

  • The instinct to avoid beeps even though beeps are the only source of information — and how long it takes the group to reframe mistakes as progress.
  • How the team gathers input and decides the next step: does it genuinely vote, quietly follow one person, or fragment into competing directions?
  • How teammates treat the person currently on the maze — the support, or the blame, aimed at whoever is taking the risk for the group.
  • Who the group relies on to remember the path, and whether that shared memory holds up under pressure or quietly breaks down.
  • Perspective gaps: the person on the stones sees the problem very differently from those coaching at the edge, and that gap shapes the tone of the whole team.
  • Whether the group stays focused on the process of learning the path, or fixates only on reaching the other side.

Variations

Several small changes sharpen different lessons. Put the team on silence during planning to expose how much it leans on constant chatter. Blindfold some members so they must be coached across entirely by others. Run more than one team on the same stones, moving in different directions, to add coordination pressure. And instead of an automatic penalty for a wrong step, let the person who beeped choose between a small forfeit and a time penalty — a quiet test of how far an individual, and the group, will go to reach the goal.

Who it's for

Learning Maze suits groups of 8 to 36 and runs indoors or outdoors in about 45 to 60 minutes including the debrief. Because the energy is low and the demands are mental rather than physical, it works well for mixed groups, seated-heavy rooms, and teams you want to coach through how they handle unforeseen situations, setbacks and the people who surface them. Choose it when your goal is to turn the negative fallout of a decision into a shared learning experience that shapes the next one.

Questions to spark the debrief

The activity is the vehicle; the debrief is where the lesson lands. Questions we ask:

  • Why did you try to avoid the beeps when the beeps are the only way to know the path? Where else do we do that, and why?
  • What was more important — the process or the result? Explain.
  • What could the beeps represent in your real working life, and who makes them?
  • How did we treat the people who found the beeps, and how do we treat the people who find mistakes at work?
  • What did you learn here that you could apply to a similar situation on the job?

Frequently asked questions

How many people is Learning Maze for?
Learning Maze is built for 8–36 people. We can scale the setup up or down to fit your group.

How long does Learning Maze take?
Allow about 45–60 min (incl. debrief). Most of the real value comes from the debrief that follows.

Can Learning Maze be run indoors or outdoors?
Indoors or outdoors.

What does Learning Maze teach a team?
It focuses on coaching and communication, and the debrief connects what happens in the activity directly to how your team works day to day.
