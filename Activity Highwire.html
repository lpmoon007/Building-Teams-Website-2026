Highwire

Highwire is a high-energy team building activity for groups of 8–16 people.

Group size8–16 people
EnergyHigh
Time45–60 min (incl. debrief)
SettingIndoors or outdoors
Best for
CoachingCommunicationCritical ThinkingLeadershipProblem SolvingTrust

What it is

Highwire is a hands-on physical challenge with a simple-sounding goal and a lot of trust riding on it: get every member of the team past a raised line — the “wire” — without anyone touching it. Nobody can go under it, nobody can go around it, and if a person makes contact the team resets and starts that crossing again. Because people can’t clear it alone, the group has to physically support, lift and spot one another, which turns an ordinary room or lawn into a real test of planning, trust and coordination.

What makes Highwire such a strong team activity is that success is impossible without the whole group. The first person over is easy; the question is who goes next, who anchors, who gets lifted, and — the hardest part — how the very last person gets across once there’s no one left on the near side to help. It rewards a team that plans the whole sequence before it moves, and it exposes one that charges in.

How it works

The team is given a defined boundary to cross — a rope or line set at a challenging height — and a single rule that changes everything: no contact with the wire, by anyone, at any time. The group has to figure out the order, the lifts and the spotting, then execute the crossing as one unit, resetting whenever someone touches. We tailor the height, the space and the safety setup to your group and venue, and our facilitators handle all of the spotting and safety coaching on the day. (Contact us and we’ll walk you through exactly how we’d run it for your team.)

What it reveals: trust

Highwire asks people to physically put their safety in a teammate’s hands — to be lifted, steadied and caught — and to take responsibility for someone else’s in return. That’s a far more visceral version of the trust every team runs on at work: the willingness to depend on a colleague, and to be the person others can depend on. Watching who volunteers to be lifted first, who hangs back, and how carefully the spotters take their job says a great deal about where a team’s trust actually sits.

What it reveals: problem solving & planning

The puzzle isn’t getting one person over — it’s sequencing the whole team, and especially solving for the last person with no helper left behind. Teams that pause to map the full order before touching anyone tend to sail through; teams that start lifting and improvise usually strand themselves and have to reset. It’s a fast, physical lesson in thinking a process all the way through before committing to it — exactly the gap that shows up in projects that launch before they’re planned.

What it reveals: communication & leadership

Coordinating a lift takes clear, calm, real-time communication — counts, cues, and someone willing to direct traffic without bulldozing the group. Highwire almost always surfaces a leader, and it’s revealing to watch how that happens: does the person with a plan get heard, does the group rally behind them, and does leadership shift as the crossing gets harder? The quieter voices matter too, because the person being lifted often has the best view of what’s actually working.

Interesting things to watch for

  • How the team solves for the last person — the moment that separates a planned crossing from an improvised one.
  • Who volunteers to be lifted first, and who waits to be asked — a quiet read on trust and comfort with vulnerability.
  • Whether the group maps the full order before it starts moving, or charges in and resets.
  • How carefully the spotters treat their responsibility once they realize a teammate’s safety is in their hands.
  • How the team reacts to a reset — frustration and blame, or a calm regroup and a better plan.

Who it’s for

Highwire suits teams of roughly 8–16 who are up for a physical, high-energy challenge and want to build genuine trust alongside problem solving. It works indoors or outdoors, needs a facilitator for safe spotting, and lands especially well with groups that talk a good game about “having each other’s backs” and want to feel what that actually takes.

Questions to spark the debrief

The activity is the vehicle; the debrief is where the lesson lands. Questions we ask:

  • How did it feel to depend on the team to get you across — and to be responsible for someone else?
  • Did the team plan the whole crossing before moving, or figure it out as you went? What did that cost or save you?
  • How did you solve for the last person, and what did that require from everyone?
  • What kind of leadership emerged, and did it change as the challenge got harder?
  • Where does “trusting the team to catch you” show up in how you work together day to day?
