Who Are You?

By James Carter · Updated July 2026

In ten minutes, two near-strangers say more about what they truly believe than they’ve told most of their friends.

Group size8+ people
EnergyLow
Time45–60 min (incl. debrief)
SettingIndoors or outdoors
Best for
TrustCommunicationLeadership

By James Carter, founder of Building Teams — 25+ years designing team-building experiences for hundreds of leadership teams. Updated July 2026.

What it is

Who Are You? is a paired activity that walks two people through three deepening levels of communication — and shows them how good it feels to talk on a genuinely deeper level with someone else. It is deceptively simple on paper. In practice it is one of the best opening activities there is: partners routinely learn more about each other in a few minutes than they have in years of working side by side.

It is less a team task than a communication and connection experience. There is nothing to build and nothing to win — just a question, asked over and over, that pulls people past small talk and into what they actually value. It energizes a room, breaks down the ‘stranger’ barrier fast, and sets a tone of openness that the rest of a session can stand on. Do not be fooled by how childish it looks at the start; facilitators use it in large conference settings to great effect.

How it works

Pairs take turns answering one question — “Who are you?” — on repeat for sixty seconds at a stretch. The rounds climb through three levels. Level 1, What I Do, is the résumé layer: titles, roles, the external facts (“I am a parent”). Level 2, Who I Am, moves inward to behaviors and character (“I am a patient parent”). Level 3, What I Believe, is pure intangibles — the values and beliefs that drive us (“I am a parent who believes every child should go to college”). Partners switch after each level, so most people move through the levels with more than one person. The whole thing runs in about ten to fifteen minutes. (Full step-by-step setup, timing and debrief questions are in the downloadable guide below.)

What it reveals: communication

Most workplace conversation lives at Level 1. We introduce ourselves by title, we report what we did, we trade tangible facts — and then we wonder why colleagues who have shared an office for years still feel like strangers. This activity makes the ceiling of surface-level talk obvious, because it forces people up past it. The jump from “what I do” to “what I believe” is the same jump that separates a transaction from a relationship, and once people feel it, they recognize how rarely they make it on purpose. The lesson lands hardest for anyone whose job is connection: what would change if salespeople reached Level 3 with customers, or leaders with the people they manage, instead of staying safely on the surface?

What it reveals: trust

Level 3 asks people to be a little vulnerable — to say out loud what they actually believe, to someone they barely know. What surprises participants is how quickly that feels safe, and how good it feels. Disclosure invites disclosure; when one partner is honest, the other follows, and a bond forms in minutes that would normally take months. That is the quiet engine of trust on a real team: not shared tasks, but the willingness to be known. The activity gives everyone a small, low-risk taste of it, and a reason to seek it out more often.

Interesting things to watch for

  • The energy of the room lifts as the levels deepen — a hush of concentration, then a rise in laughter and animation as people hit Level 3.
  • The kind of answers changes sharply between levels. Jot down examples; they make the debrief vivid and are proof, in the group’s own words, of how differently people talk when the frame shifts.
  • Some people struggle to leave Level 1 — they keep listing roles and facts even when asked for beliefs. It is a telling habit worth naming gently.
  • Watch for anyone not really participating or treating it as a joke. If that is widespread, pause and explain the three levels so the group sees where it is headed.
  • Listen at the end for the classic reaction: “I just told a stranger things I’ve never told my closest friends.” It happens almost every time.

Variations

Two variations are worth knowing. Front-load it: tell the group up front that within ten minutes they will tell a complete stranger more about their values and beliefs than they have told most of their friends. They won’t believe you — and reminding them of the promise at the end is what makes the point land. Explain the three levels at the start: with a skeptical or difficult group, laying out all three levels first buys their buy-in, because Level 1 feels so superficial that some groups need to know the activity is actually going somewhere.

Who it’s for

Who Are You? shines as an opener for groups of eight or more, indoors or out, and works just as well at a large conference as in a small team room. It needs no equipment and only an even number of people. Choose it when you want to warm up a room fast, dissolve the awkwardness between people who don’t know each other, or set a tone of openness and trust for everything that follows.

Questions to spark the debrief

The activity is the vehicle; the debrief is where the lesson lands. Questions we ask:

  • What did you think about this activity when we started, and what do you think about it now?
  • What were some of the topics that came up?
  • How did you feel during Level Three communication?
  • How often do you engage in Level Three communication in your daily life?
  • What would happen if you spoke with your customers (or your children, or your team) using Level Three communication on a more regular basis?
  • What can you do to remind yourself to engage in more Level Three communication?

Frequently asked questions

How many people is Who Are You? for?
Who Are You? is built for 8+ people. We can scale the setup up or down to fit your group.

How long does Who Are You? take?
Allow about 45–60 min (incl. debrief). Most of the real value comes from the debrief that follows.

Can Who Are You? be run indoors or outdoors?
Indoors or outdoors.

What does Who Are You? teach a team?
It focuses on trust, communication and leadership, and the debrief connects what happens in the activity directly to how your team works day to day.
