Win Win Win

Over five rounds, teams discover that competitive spirit only carries them so far — and collaboration toward a common goal wins far bigger.

Group size15–35 people
EnergyHigh
Time45–60 min (incl. debrief)
SettingIndoors or outdoors
Best for
CommunicationCollaborationLeadershipProblem Solving

By James Carter, founder of Building Teams — 25+ years designing team-building experiences for hundreds of leadership teams. Updated July 2026.

What it is: collaboration vs. competition

What should a team’s strategy actually be — win, or help everyone win? Win Win Win hands a room full of people a simple brief from the top — maximize profit over the next quarter — splits them into five teams, and turns them loose to grab colored balls that stand in for that profit. What happens next is almost always the same: heads down, teams raid the center and each other, and the total profit stays stubbornly low. It takes a few rounds and a pointed question or two before the room realizes the game was never five teams against each other. It was one organization against its own instincts.

That realization is the whole point. Win Win Win is a fast, loud, high-energy game that proves — in numbers on a flip chart, not slogans — that competitive spirit carries a team only so far, and that working toward a common goal yields dramatically better results. It teaches collaboration both within teams and across them, which is exactly why it lands so hard when the five teams are named after real departments in your own organization.

How it works

Five teams arrange themselves around a large central loop, each with a home base of their own. Over a series of timed rounds, players dash in to collect balls — one at a time — from the center or from any other team’s base, carrying each one home before going back for more. The balls score like a poker hand: a “full house” of the right colors is worth the most, and the wrong combinations actually cost points. Left alone, teams treat it as a scramble for a fixed pile and end up sabotaging one another. The magic variation is to total every team’s score into a single organizational result — and suddenly the optimal play is for everyone to help everyone fill a full house. (Full step-by-step setup, scoring, the storyline and round-by-round facilitator cues are in the downloadable guide below.)

What it reveals: competition vs. collaboration

Most teams arrive with a reflex to compete, and this game exposes it in minutes. Told to “maximize profit,” people hear beat the other teams — so they hoard, raid rivals’ bases, and guard their own, and the grand total stays low even as everyone sprints. The uncomfortable insight is that no one broke the rules; they simply assumed the wrong goal. When the facilitator later adds up all five scores and asks how the CEO would feel about that as the company’s real quarterly profit, the silence does the teaching. Teams leave understanding, viscerally, that competing over a shared resource shrinks the pie for everyone while a common goal expands it — the exact dynamic that plays out between siloed departments back at the office.

What it reveals: leadership

Watch closely in the early rounds and you’ll almost always spot one or two people who see the answer early — “wait, we could all win if we just share” — and try to say so. Usually they get drowned out by the noise and the running. That moment is the leadership lesson in miniature: the willingness to rise above your own team’s score for the good of the whole, and the harder challenge of getting a distracted, competitive group to actually listen. The debrief often circles back to those people — what were they trying to tell everyone, and why didn’t it land? — which opens an honest conversation about whose good ideas go unheard at work when everyone is heads-down and busy.

What it reveals: problem solving and communication

On the surface the puzzle is the scoring: which combinations pay, which ones penalize, and how to organize a scramble to hit them. But the real problem can’t be solved inside any single team — only the whole room talking to each other can solve it, and the clock never quite leaves enough time to strategize comfortably. Teams that pause to communicate a shared plan outperform teams that simply move faster. The contrast is unforgettable by the final round: once the group coordinates, they complete the task in seconds and then stand around with time to spare, a stark reversal from the frantic early rounds. It sets up the facilitator’s favorite question — what could you have done with all that extra time at work?

Interesting things to watch for

  • The instinctive raid — how fast teams start taking balls from each other rather than from the shared center, and whether anyone stops to question it.
  • The lone collaborator — the person who proposes “let’s all just help each other” early, and whether the group listens or steamrolls them.
  • Creative rule interpretation — teams read the rules aggressively in their own favor; watch for it and let the other teams weigh in on whether it’s fair.
  • Quiet cheating — palming balls during scoring or holding them back — a revealing signal of how the pressure to “win” can bend behavior.
  • The base constraint — someone must always stay home, so watch how teams split roles between gatherers and guards under time pressure.
  • The round-five reversal — a coordinated group finishes in a fraction of the time and then waits, idle; that leftover time is the lesson.

Variations

The game flexes to sharpen whichever lesson you’re after:

  • Combined scoring: total all five teams’ results into one organizational number each round — the cleanest way to defuse competition and make the collaborative optimum obvious.
  • Name the teams: call them after real departments or functions — sales, operations, IT, admin — and rewrite the storyline to fit your organization, so the parallels are impossible to dodge in the debrief.
  • Go big and outside: place the teams 15–20 yards apart outdoors to make strategy harder and turn it into a genuine running exercise.
  • Hidden center: use a bucket instead of an open loop for the central balls so teams can’t see what’s available before they commit.
  • Energy dial: have participants walk for a medium-energy session, or run for a high-energy one.
  • Scale the teams: add or remove teams to fit your group size without changing the core dynamic.

Who it’s for

Win Win Win is built for larger groups — roughly 15 to 35 people — and runs indoors or outdoors at high energy in about 45 to 60 minutes including the debrief. It’s the right choice when you want to build pride in joint effort and expose silo behavior between teams or departments, demonstrating in both monetary and human terms what an organization gains when its people collaborate for the common good instead of competing over limited resources.

Questions to spark the debrief

The activity is the vehicle; the debrief is where the lesson lands. Questions we ask:

  • What was happening during the exercise?
  • What was the goal of the activity?
  • There were five teams — but you all work for the same organization, in the same department, right? So let’s add these scores together.
  • How do you feel about the score for the entire group?
  • How would the CEO or Executive Director feel if these were the real profits for your organization?
  • The goal was to maximize profit — so what was the total potential profit we left on the table?
  • What would happen if you got these results on a continual basis, and why did it happen?
  • Do we have a common goal — and where is this same competitiveness playing out in our organization right now?

Frequently asked questions

How many people is Win Win Win for?
Win Win Win is built for 15–35 people. We can scale the setup up or down to fit your group.

How long does Win Win Win take?
Allow about 45–60 min (incl. debrief). Most of the real value comes from the debrief that follows.

Can Win Win Win be run indoors or outdoors?
Indoors or outdoors.

What does Win Win Win teach a team?
It focuses on communication, collaboration, leadership and problem solving, and the debrief connects what happens in the activity directly to how your team works day to day.
