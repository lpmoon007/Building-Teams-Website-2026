Don't Touch Me

By James Carter · Updated July 2026

Two rules, a small pad on the floor — and a team that keeps tripping over the rules it invented itself.

Group size8–40 people
EnergyMedium
Time45–60 min (incl. debrief)
SettingAnywhere
Best for
CommunicationCritical ThinkingLeadershipProblem SolvingQuality Improvement

By James Carter, founder of Building Teams — 25+ years designing team-building experiences for hundreds of leadership teams. Updated July 2026.

See it in action

What it is

Don’t Touch Me is almost embarrassingly simple to set up — a small pad on the ground, a circle of people, and two rules — yet it produces one of the sharpest ‘aha’ moments we run. The group forms a circle around the pad, each person picks a partner across from them, and the task is this: everyone must touch the pad and switch positions with their partner, as fast as possible. That’s it. Most teams take the better part of a minute on their first attempt. With some out-of-the-box thinking, most get under ten seconds — and one group (a third-grade class, no less) once cracked it in under a single second.

The real subject of the exercise isn’t the pad. It’s the pile of rules the team invents that were never in the instructions — stand in a circle, run one at a time, keep your original spot. Warning: this event can trigger a genuine paradigm shift. The moment a team notices its own self-imposed rules, the solutions come flooding in. If you want a group to feel — not just hear about — the difference between the rules they were given and the rules they assumed, this is the game.

How it works

The team stands in a circle around a small “Don’t Touch Me” pad, and each person pairs with someone across the circle. On the timekeeper’s go, everyone must touch the pad and swap places with their partner, and the clock stops when the whole group has done it. There are only two real rules — touch the pad, switch with your partner — and the facilitator refuses to add any more, no matter how many the group asks for. Teams run it repeatedly, shaving seconds each round as they abandon the constraints they invented for themselves. (Full step-by-step setup, the fastest known solutions, and debrief questions are in the downloadable guide below.)

What it reveals: self-imposed limits and quality improvement

What makes this activity unusual is that the team isn’t solving whether it can be done — it’s solving how to do it far better than it currently does. Nearly every barrier the group hits is one it built itself: assuming ‘switch positions’ means physically walking across the circle, assuming people must go one at a time, assuming the shape can’t change. Peel those away and a sixty-second task becomes a five-second one. That is exactly the work of continuous improvement at the office — the process wasn’t handed down on stone tablets; someone assumed it had to run this way. The debrief question writes itself: what implied rules at work are quietly capping how good we could be?

What it reveals: communication

Because there are so many possible solutions to weigh, communication is the whole ballgame. Good ideas get discarded — and it’s often not the idea that fails but the way it was said. It also becomes plain, fast, that a few individuals tend to own the conversation while others get talked over. The most revealing pattern is the quiet person whose suggestion is met with silence — no one even acknowledges they spoke, and the idea evaporates. Teams recognise this instantly because it happens in their real meetings every week. Run inside the safety of a game, it becomes a topic a team can actually name and decide to change.

What it reveals: leadership and critical thinking

Watch who steps up, and watch how the group treats the ideas that don’t come from them. Leadership here is less about volume and more about whether the person driving can hold the group’s attention on the actual constraints instead of the imagined ones. Critical thinking and problem solving are almost one and the same in this event: the breakthrough is a reframe, not a calculation. And when a team beats ten seconds and declares a ‘world record,’ there’s a second lesson waiting — why measure yourself against the entire world to feel satisfied, rather than against your own last attempt?

Interesting things to watch for

  • The group repeatedly asks the facilitator for permission — “do we have to stand in a circle?” — and only makes real progress once it accepts that no more direction is coming.
  • Teams trying the same bad idea over and over, expecting a different result.
  • Good ideas shot down before they get a fair hearing — and how the person whose idea was dismissed reacts afterward.
  • Whether anyone in the group speaks up for an overlooked teammate, or lets it slide.
  • A visible climb in creativity once the first self-imposed rule is broken — suddenly nothing feels off-limits.
  • Groups getting so inventive they attempt something physically unsafe — a cue for the facilitator to keep the space safe without handing them the answer.

Variations

  • Silence the leader. Silence the first person to speak — frequently the group’s natural leader — and watch who fills the vacuum.
  • Blindfolds or selective silence. Blindfold or mute a few participants to change how information has to move through the team.
  • Name a leader first. Choose a leader before the event starts and observe the decision-making that follows.
  • Add observers. Pull two people out to watch and take notes, then feed their observations into the debrief.
  • Chase the clock. Let the team keep re-running it to drive the time down until they’re satisfied — the improvement curve itself becomes a talking point.

Who it’s for

Don’t Touch Me works for groups of 8 to 40, indoors or out, and needs only a small prop and enough open floor to experiment. It’s medium energy and runs in about 45 to 60 minutes including the debrief. It’s an ideal fit for teams talking about innovation, process improvement, meeting dynamics, or any team convinced that “this is just how we do it here.”

Questions to spark the debrief

The activity is the vehicle; the debrief is where the lesson lands. Questions we ask:

  • What process did you use to arrive at your solution?
  • Were some good ideas overlooked? Are there other areas in life where good ideas are being overlooked or never even considered?
  • How do you feel about your final time compared to your first — and what does it take for a team to improve that much?
  • What was the single greatest challenge you, or the group, faced in this activity?
  • What implied rules kept you from finding the best solution — and what implied rules at work or at home keep you from being your best?
  • How can you take what you learned here and apply it back to daily situations?

Frequently asked questions

How many people is Don't Touch Me for?
Don't Touch Me is built for 8–40 people. We can scale the setup up or down to fit your group.

How long does Don't Touch Me take?
Allow about 45–60 min (incl. debrief). Most of the real value comes from the debrief that follows.

Can Don't Touch Me be run indoors or outdoors?
Anywhere.

What does Don't Touch Me teach a team?
It focuses on communication, critical thinking, leadership, problem solving and quality improvement, and the debrief connects what happens in the activity directly to how your team works day to day.
