Change Five

By James Carter · Updated July 2026

A deceptively silly icebreaker that lets a team feel, in real time, how hard it is to make a change stick — and how fast we snap back to comfortable.

Group size8+ people
EnergyLow
Time45–60 min (incl. debrief)
SettingIndoors or outdoors
Best for
ChangeProblem SolvingCompetition vs Collaboration

By James Carter, founder of Building Teams — 25+ years designing team-building experiences for hundreds of leadership teams. Updated July 2026.

What it is

Change Five is a fast, funny icebreaker with a serious idea hiding inside it. Everyone pairs up, studies their partner for a few seconds, then turns back-to-back and changes five things about their appearance — an untucked shirt, a watch swapped to the other wrist, glasses pushed up on the head, shoes switched. Then they do it again. And again. On the surface it is light and a little ridiculous, and the room fills with laughter. Underneath, it is one of the cleanest demonstrations we know of a truth most teams never say out loud: nearly everyone claims to welcome change, and nearly everyone quietly resists it.

Because it needs no equipment and only twenty to thirty minutes, Change Five works best as the opening move before a conversation about change — a reorganization, a new process, a merger, a system rollout. It gives a group a shared, physical experience of resistance that you can point back to for the rest of the day: remember how it felt around change number twelve?

How it works

Participants find a partner and take fifteen seconds to memorize how the other person looks. Standing back-to-back, they get thirty seconds to change five things about their own appearance, then turn around and spot what changed. Without putting anything back, they repeat the round — five more changes, then five more — until each person has changed fifteen things and is looking distinctly rumpled. What happens next, once the facilitator appears to wrap up, is the moment the whole activity is built around. (Full step-by-step setup, the facilitator’s closing move, and the complete debrief are in the downloadable guide below.)

What it reveals: resistance to change

The first five changes are effortless. The second five take real thought. By the third round people are borrowing ideas, stalling, and audibly groaning — and that arc, felt in the body, is the lesson. Change is easy while it is optional and cheap, and it gets harder the deeper it cuts into what feels normal. The most powerful reveal comes at the end: the instant the group thinks the exercise is over, almost everyone immediately puts their clothes and accessories back exactly the way they started — even though no one asked them to. We gravitate toward what is comfortable and familiar, and we do it without noticing. That snap-back is precisely what happens with a new workflow or org change: people comply while they are watched, then quietly revert to the old way the moment the pressure lifts. Naming that tendency early makes it far easier to catch later.

What it reveals: creative problem solving

Fifteen changes is more than the obvious options allow. Once the watch, the shoes and the untucked shirt are used up, participants have to get inventive — rolling a sleeve, parting their hair the other way, adding something rather than only removing it. It is a small, safe lesson in how constraints force creativity: the answers that felt impossible a minute ago appear only once the easy ones run out. Teams that push through discover, often to their surprise, just how much they can change when they stop assuming they have hit the limit.

What it reveals: image, permission and working together

Watch closely and you will see people glance around before they commit — checking whether it is safe to look silly. This is what our facilitators call the Fimage Factor: fear of image, the very human worry about how we appear to others. When maintaining that image matters more than learning, people hold back, make half-hearted changes, and miss the fun. This is where the exercise turns from solo to shared: partners feed each other ideas, the boldest people give everyone else permission to loosen up, and a group that starts self-conscious ends up laughing together. It is a low-stakes preview of a real workplace dynamic — whether a team competes to look composed and in control, or collaborates by making it safe for each other to try, fumble and change.

Interesting things to watch for

  • How fast people snap back to their original appearance the moment they think the activity is over — though no one told them to.
  • Half-hearted attempts — who is protecting their image rather than diving in?
  • Especially creative or extreme changes, and how they give the rest of the group permission to stretch.
  • Whether people only remove things, or think to add and rearrange — a tell for how narrowly they frame the problem.
  • Who looks around at others before changing, and who just commits.
  • How the mood shifts from stiff and self-conscious to loose and laughing as the rounds go on.

Variations

  • Change more or fewer than five each round to raise or lower the difficulty.
  • Keep going — up to twenty-five changes. It gets genuinely hard and drives home how much we are actually capable of changing.
  • Have the facilitator make the same changes as the group and keep them on to the very end — looking funny in front of everyone gives participants permission to stretch past their fear of image.
  • Let people walk around afterward and point out the changes their teammates made — a fun, energizing close.

Who it’s for

Change Five suits groups of eight or more (you need an even number so everyone has a partner) and runs equally well indoors or outdoors, seated or standing, in about twenty to thirty minutes. It needs nothing but a little imagination, which makes it ideal as an icebreaker to open a retreat, workshop or meeting — especially any session where change, adaptation or a new way of working is on the agenda.

Questions to spark the debrief

The activity is the vehicle; the debrief is where the lesson lands. Questions we ask:

  • What did you notice in the group? Did you look around at other people?
  • Did you think you had to take five things off, or did you add anything to your outfit?
  • Did you resist the changes? Why or why not?
  • Did the changes get easier or harder as you went? Why?
  • Do you see this same mentality showing up in other parts of your life or work?
  • How do these realities shape the way we respond to change?
  • What is one thing you could do differently to be less resistant to change?

Frequently asked questions

How many people is Change Five for?
Change Five is built for 8+ people. We can scale the setup up or down to fit your group.

How long does Change Five take?
Allow about 45–60 min (incl. debrief). Most of the real value comes from the debrief that follows.

Can Change Five be run indoors or outdoors?
Indoors or outdoors.

What does Change Five teach a team?
It focuses on change, problem solving and competition vs collaboration, and the debrief connects what happens in the activity directly to how your team works day to day.
