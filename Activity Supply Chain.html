Supply Chain

By James Carter · Updated July 2026

Every person is one link in the chain — and the whole team feels it the moment a single marble hits the floor.

Group size8–32 people
EnergyMedium
Time45–60 min (incl. debrief)
SettingInside and Outside
Best for
CommunicationProblem SolvingQuality Improvement

By James Carter, founder of Building Teams — 25+ years designing team-building experiences for hundreds of leadership teams. Updated July 2026.

What it is

Supply Chain physically demonstrates a metaphor every organization talks about but few teams ever feel: that each member is an important ‘link in the chain.’ Each person holds a single length of track, and together the group has to move marbles from one end of a production line to the other — through every person’s link, in sequence, without a hand ever touching a marble and without a marble ever hitting the floor. Drop one, and it goes back to the start. Skip a link, and it doesn’t count.

It looks almost trivially simple, which is exactly the trap. The task itself is easy; performing it with speed, quality and consistency is not — and the group’s own habits, unwritten rules and shortcuts are usually what get in the way. It is a ‘pure fun’ exercise on the surface with a surprisingly serious set of lessons underneath, and it runs equally well indoors or outside.

How it works

The group is cast as workers in a gumball factory racing to cut production time before the holiday rush. Marbles (the gumballs) must travel from one loading dock to the other along the human supply chain of individual tracks. If a marble is touched by a hand or falls to the ground, it’s contaminated or rejected and returns to the start. A short ‘staff meeting’ to plan, then the line goes live — usually against a clock, and often against a target the team sets for itself. (Full step-by-step setup, the storyline, rules and debrief questions are in the downloadable guide below.)

What it reveals: communication

A supply chain only moves as well as the hand-offs between its links. The moment production starts, communication becomes the bottleneck: people have to signal when they’re ready, call the pace, warn the next link, and agree on how a marble actually transfers from one track to the next. When it’s smooth, the line hums; when it isn’t, marbles hit the floor and everyone points at the gap. The debrief question we always come back to — “Was communication excellent, good, okay, or bad?” — lands hard because the answer is sitting on the floor in front of them. It’s a direct mirror of the hand-offs between people, shifts and departments at work, where a dropped ball is rarely one person’s fault and almost always a communication one.

What it reveals: problem solving

There is no single right structure, so the team has to invent one — a line, a circle, a tight cluster — and then discover its flaws in real time. Watch what happens when the first plan doesn’t work: often, new ideas get squelched precisely because they’re new and the clock is running. Leadership frequently shifts once planning ends and execution begins, exposing the people who love to plan but stall on the details — and the ones who only come alive once there’s something to do. Comparing the different structures a group tries, and asking whether they see the same pattern at work, is one of the most revealing conversations the activity produces.

What it reveals: quality and integrity

This is where a ‘simple game’ gets honest. Under a deadline, teams start trading quality for quantity: they rush, they cut corners, and some quietly bend the rules — a finger steadying a marble here, a skipped link there — to keep production moving. It surfaces the uncomfortable truth that individuals and groups will sacrifice integrity to hit the larger goal. If people will cut corners over marbles, what happens at work when the stakes are real? The exercise creates a safe, low-consequence space to name that dynamic out loud, and to talk about what it costs a team when quality and honesty are the first things to go under pressure.

Interesting things to watch for

  • How the group takes an idea and actually puts it into action — and how long it clings to a plan that clearly isn’t working.
  • Whether people are following the rules, or cheating in small ways to avoid stopping production.
  • Group norms and unwritten ‘rules’ that quietly cap the team’s production and enjoyment.
  • Rising frustration — and team members who become disassociated and drift to the edge of the process.
  • How the group responds to a dropped marble: blame, a shrug, a fix, or a rethink?
  • The physical structure the group chooses (circle, line, cluster) — and whether they recognize it as a version of how they organize at work.

Variations

  • For a harder version, don’t allow the tracks to touch when transferring a marble.
  • Allow each participant to use only one hand.
  • Swap in a lighter object (a bead or bean) or a heavier one (a small steel ball).
  • Have two people share a single track.
  • Put the team on silence, or blindfold one or more members.
  • Put the tracks very close together — surprisingly, bunching everyone up makes it harder, not easier.
  • Set the goal two ways: how fast can you move six gumballs, or how many can you produce in fifteen minutes? Ask the group to predict the number first — they almost always overestimate.

Who it’s for

Any group that would benefit from seeing, physically, how every person plays a valuable and necessary role. It’s especially good for showing interdepartmental dependence — sales, admin, warehouse — and it’s a natural fit for process-oriented organizations like manufacturing, where the question “how do we make our process faster without skipping steps?” is the whole job. Works for groups of 8 to 32, indoors or out.

Questions to spark the debrief

The activity is the vehicle; the debrief is where the lesson lands. Questions we ask:

  • What qualities did your team need to be successful, and where else could you use those same qualities?
  • Did you agree on a start-to-finish plan — and if so, did you actually follow it?
  • Did the plan work? If not, what was at fault, and what did you do to compensate?
  • Was communication excellent, good, okay, or bad? Could it have been better, and how?
  • How did it feel when someone dropped a ball — and what did you notice about the team’s reaction to a drop?
  • Do you see any correlations between this activity and what your group faces on a regular basis?

Frequently asked questions

How many people is Supply Chain for?
Supply Chain is built for 8–32 people. We can scale the setup up or down to fit your group.

How long does Supply Chain take?
Allow about 45–60 min (incl. debrief). Most of the real value comes from the debrief that follows.
