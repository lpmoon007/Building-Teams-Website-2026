Knot or NOT a Knot

A deceptively simple choice — knot or no knot — that exposes how a group really makes, defends and follows a decision.

Group size4–36 people
EnergyLow
Time45–60 min (incl. debrief)
SettingInside and Outside
Best for
ChangeCommunicationCompetition vs CollaborationDecision MakingLeadershipProblem Solving

By James Carter, founder of Building Teams — 25+ years designing team-building experiences for hundreds of leadership teams. Updated July 2026.

See it in action

What it is

A rope lies bundled on the floor, its two ends poking out on opposite sides. The facilitator says one sentence, once: your group has to decide whether — when the rope is finally pulled tight — there will be a knot in it, or no knot. People split. Some walk to the ‘knot’ side, some to the ‘no knot’ side, and almost nobody notices the word that actually matters: group. From that moment on, the exercise stops being about a rope at all.

Knot or NOT a Knot is a low-energy decision-making activity that simulates how we choose — both when we have time to deliberate and when the clock is running out. It challenges people to make a call and stand by it, to genuinely understand and accept the decisions of others, and to see the whole picture instead of clinging to a single piece of “critical information.” Along the way it surfaces the uncomfortable questions every team eventually has to ask itself: Am I digging my heels in because I’m right, or because I’m in love with my own idea?

How it works

Working with a partner, the facilitator slowly draws the two ends of the rope apart while the group, divided into two camps, tries to convince the other side that their prediction is correct. At set points — roughly a third of the way, then again near the end — people are invited to switch sides based on the “evidence” and the other camp’s arguments, until a final window closes and everyone must commit. Then the rope is pulled tight and the truth is revealed. The catch: whether a knot appears is essentially irrelevant to the real challenge, which was for the group to decide together. (Full step-by-step setup, the exact wording of the challenge, and debrief questions are in the downloadable guide below.)

What it reveals: decision making

The instant the challenge is spoken, watch what happens: individuals make a private call and physically plant themselves on a side. Nobody was told to decide alone — they simply defaulted to it. That default is the whole point. Most workplace decisions get made the same way, from personal perception, before anyone has stepped back to ask whether this was even an individual’s decision to make. The activity separates two things people constantly confuse: being right and having group support. It is entirely possible to guess the rope correctly and still have failed the actual task — and that gap is where the most honest conversations of the day begin.

What it reveals: competition vs collaboration

Splitting into ‘knot’ and ‘no knot’ turns a group into two teams almost instantly, and with it comes the urge to win the argument rather than solve the problem. People lobby, they persuade, they defend their corner — all while the answer that would have satisfied the challenge was for everyone to stand together. It is a small, vivid model of what happens when departments, factions or personalities compete over a decision that was never meant to have losing sides. Every so often a group catches it and unites; more often they discover, in the debrief, how readily manufactured competition crowded out the collaboration that was available the entire time.

What it reveals: leadership, change and the courage to stand alone

This exercise quietly tests two opposite muscles at once. When someone privately suspects the rope will land the opposite way from their side, will they say so — or go quiet and follow the crowd? And when new “evidence” appears as the rope is drawn out, can people change their minds without feeling they’ve lost face? Leadership shows up in who names the real challenge, who invites others to switch, and who makes it safe to be persuaded. It can be genuinely difficult to put ego aside and be a truly selfless team member, and this activity lets participants feel that tension authentically — without anyone being hit over the head for being stubborn.

Interesting things to watch for

  • How fast people default to deciding alone — nobody instructed them to, yet the group fractures into sides within seconds.
  • Who quietly suspected the group was wrong and said nothing — and what it took to go along with the crowd anyway.
  • Whether switching sides is treated as smart re-evaluation or as “losing” — watch the body language when someone crosses over.
  • The rare group that catches the word group and unites — and whether that was one person’s insight or a shared realisation.
  • The moment someone starts arguing to win rather than to find the truth.
  • Participants who insist afterward that they were tricked or misled — a revealing window into how people handle being caught out.

Variations

  • Presentation round. After the first pull, give each side a few minutes to build a case and present their reasons to the other camp — raising the stakes on persuasion and competition.
  • One decision only. Let participants choose a side once, at the very beginning, with no chance to switch — so they feel the consequences of an uninformed, locked-in decision.
  • Free movement. Allow people to move between sides at will throughout, except during the final third — changing how commitment and second-guessing play out.

Who it’s for

Knot or NOT a Knot suits groups of 4 to 36, runs indoors or out, and needs only about 45 to 60 minutes including the debrief. Because it is low-energy and rewards reflection over athletics, it works especially well as a closing challenge — a way to test a team that has already gelled and leave them with something to chew on about how they really make and defend decisions.

Questions to spark the debrief

The activity is the vehicle; the debrief is where the lesson lands. Questions we ask:

  • Who feels they were successful in this activity — and why?
  • Why did you automatically make the decision about the rope as an individual instead of as a group?
  • How many of you switched sides, and what made you move?
  • How did it feel when the other side tried to convince you your decision was wrong?
  • What matters more when making a decision — being right, or having group support?
  • Did anyone suspect the rope would turn out the opposite of their side? If so, why did you go along with the group and not stand up for what you thought?
  • What does it mean to stick together as a group even when the outcome is negative?

Frequently asked questions

How many people is Knot or NOT a Knot for?
Knot or NOT a Knot is built for 4–36 people. We can scale the setup up or down to fit your group.

How long does Knot or NOT a Knot take?
Allow about 45–60 min (incl. debrief). Most of the real value comes from the debrief that follows.

What does Knot or NOT a Knot teach a team?
